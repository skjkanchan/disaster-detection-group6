# Inland Flooding in North Carolina

Although Hurricane Matthew weakened to a Category 1 by the time it reached South Carolina, the storm's most destructive U.S. impacts unfolded in the week following landfall as river systems across eastern North Carolina crested at record or near-record levels.

## Key Events

- **October 8–9, 2016** — 8 to 18 inches of rain fell across eastern North Carolina, with Fayetteville recording 14.8 inches and Elizabethtown 18.95 inches.
- **October 10, 2016** — The Lumber River crested at 24.8 ft in Lumberton, inundating roughly a third of the city and forcing the evacuation of 1,500 residents from a public-housing complex.
- **October 12, 2016** — The Neuse River crested at 29.74 ft at Kinston, the highest level on record and 15 ft above flood stage.
- **October 13, 2016** — Princeville, NC, founded by freed slaves in 1885, was evacuated and flooded for the second time in 17 years after the Tar River overtopped the town's levee.

## Casualties and Losses in North Carolina

- At least 28 confirmed deaths in North Carolina, the highest state total in the United States.
- Approximately 100,000 homes and businesses were damaged.
- State-level economic losses were estimated at $4.8 billion by the North Carolina Office of State Budget and Management.
- FEMA DR-4285-NC authorized Individual Assistance in 50 counties and Public Assistance in 45 counties.

## Long-term Recovery

The North Carolina General Assembly appropriated $200 million for Matthew recovery in 2017, and Congress included $135 million for Hurricane Matthew in the FY2017 Disaster Relief Supplemental. Recovery in Robeson, Edgecombe, and Cumberland counties continued to be a public-policy issue into 2020 — compounded by damage from Hurricane Florence (2018) in many of the same river basins.
